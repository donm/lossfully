4 # There's (at least) two ways to do this: 1) the Ruby Recipes way,
 
5 # which is to make a thread for every incoming task, but put it to
 
6 # sleep until there's room in the pool for another task, or 2) have a
 
7 # pool of threads that eat tasks from a queue.  This implements (2),
 
8 # mainly because it seemed more fun to me.  But also because it
 
9 # doesn't require the explicit use of Mutexes at all; it uses them,
 
10 # for the sake of sending signals with ConditionVaribles, but if those
 
11 # signals aren't received there would be a delay of at most 1 second.
 
12 #
 
13 # Another uses thing about this implementation is for the case when
 
14 # every task you anticipate adding to the ThreadPool of the same
 
15 # general form.  Then then ThreadPool can be initialized with a block
 
16 # and you can just add objects to the task queue.
 
17 #
